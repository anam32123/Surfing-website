Surfing

Surfing is a sport that is currently gaining popularity. Originally from the islands of Polynesia, it was brought to the rest of the world from Hawai’i. Many of the world’s best surfers still hail from Hawai’i. Surfing is unique in that it is more than a sport, it is a lifestyle and pastime. Some of the best surfers in the world do not even surf in competitions.

Competitions:

The World Surf League (WSL) is the main governing body for competitive surfing. They organize competitions around the world, including Championship Tour (CT) and Qualifying Series (QS) events. The Championship Tour is the top tier of surfing in the world, consisting of ten or eleven events per year at the best breaks and spots, that ultimately determine the world champion. The QS consists of the surfers competing for a spot on the CT. This year, the WSL became the first American sports league to offer equal prize money for men and women.

The International Surfing Association (ISA) also organizes surfing events where nation’s teams compete against each other for the world titles.

Next year, surfing will be an olympic sport for the first time!

How Surf Competitions Work

Surf competitions are usually allotted several days to run. This allows for some off days where the competition will not occur due to lower-quality surf. The entire competition usually takes around 4 days to run. Some will take more or less time. Generally, surfers surf in two-, three-, or four-person heats, with the top one or two moving on to the next round and the rest eliminated. This continues in a bracket format until there is a 2-4 surfer final heat. This format can vary depending on the competition. For example, some events have rounds without eliminations, while ones taking place in wave pools require different formats.

In heats, surfers spend an allotted period of time alone in the water trying to catch waves that will give them the highest heat score. Each wave they ride is individually scored on a scale of 1 to 10, based on factors like number and degree of difficulty of maneuvers, execution of maneuvers, commitment/risk on the wave, and wave choice. 5 judges each score the wave. Then the average of the three middle scores becomes the wave score. Then, the heat score is made up of the sum of the two highest wave scores. A set of rules called priority governs who can catch which waves, prevents surfers from stealing waves from each other, and avoids dangerous and unsportsmanlike situations. The judging and heat system can vary for specialty events and events in wave pools. The WSL also occasionally runs an overlapping heat format where multiple heats will run in the water at the same time.

Notable surfers:

Hawai’i, California, Florida, Australia, South Africa, Brazil, and France are the countries that produce some of the best surfers. See below some of the most notable surfers of all time.

Andy Irons - 3x World Champion and amazing Hawaiian surfer. He helped redefine and push forward the sport during his lifetime.

John John Florence - 2x World Champion and one of the best surfers in the world. Hailing from the North Shore of Hawai'i, he is extremely versatile and has won two of this year's CT events so far.

Carissa Moore - currently the World #1, this Hawaiian surfer has claimed the World Title 3 times and just took out her first win of the season at Jeffrey’s Bay. She is known for her aggressive and powerful surfing, as well as her work in mentoring young female surfers through her program Moore Aloha

Stephanie Gilmore - 7-time World Champion, tied for the record with fellow Australian Layne Beachley. She is notable for her stylish surfing.

Best surf spots:

There are several spots around the world notable for their excellence, whether barrels, big waves, or others.

Pipeline, Oahu, Hawai'i: widely considered to be the best wave in the world, Pipeline offers consistent, fast-paced, and heavy right-hand and left-hand barrels. While this wave on the North Shore produces some of the best rides each winter season, it is also one of the deadliest waves in the world.

Peahi (Jaws), Maui, Hawai'i: Jaws has some of the largest waves in the world.

Teahupo'o, Tahiti, French Polynesia: this is one of the heaviest waves and barrels in the world. Far from the shore, this slab produces some of the largest and most dramatic barrels.

Bells Beach, Australia: located on the Southern tip of Australia, Bells is the home of the longest running surf competition. It offers several amazing waves/breaks and the cliffs surrounding the beach and ocean provide a beautiful amphitheater setting for watching surfing.

Nazaré, Portugal: Nazaré produces the biggest waves in the world. The world records for largest waves ever ridden happened here.

Indonesia: Indonesia is home to many world-class tropical waves. It provides beautiful scenery along with pristine small- to medium-sized barrels and performance waves. The majority of Indonesia’s famous breaks, including Keramas and Uluwatu, are located on the island of Bali.
